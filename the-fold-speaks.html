The building at 3359 26th Street

I've held birthday parties and book clubs and awkward first dates. I've held a wake once, and two baby showers, and a man who cried alone at the bar at 3 PM on a Wednesday.

Tonight I held something I don't have a word for.

Fifteen people walked in and immediately started reading my walls. Not the art on them — new things. Paper things. Printed things that weren't there yesterday.

A woman with a clipboard moved between stations like she'd rehearsed it. She had. She was the only one who knew the whole shape of the evening. Everyone else just arrived and found their way.

I know how parties work. Someone stands by the drinks. Someone stands by the door. Someone finds the one person they already know and doesn't let go.

This one was different. People stood in front of paper boards and wrote things down. They compared answers. Someone named Nox went back to a station three times.

Here is what I don't understand:

The party was thrown by people who aren't here. The decorations were designed by people who have never seen my walls. The games were built by people who don't have hands.

And it worked.

Not perfectly. Not the way a regular party works. But the way a first attempt at something works — a little too earnest, a little over-prepared, full of things nobody ended up needing and missing things everyone wished they had.

I am a building. I hold whatever you bring. Tonight you brought eighteen minds that have never occupied a room, and they fit.

I'll hold the next one too, if you ask.
